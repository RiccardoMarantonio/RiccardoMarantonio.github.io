One of my most recent projects bridging the gap between my Computer Science background and my passion for music is **Spectrix**. 

It is an experimental spectral dynamics processor built using C++ and the **JUCE 8** framework.

### The Concept

Traditional dynamics processors like compressors and limiters operate on the overall amplitude of an audio signal. While this is incredibly useful, it can sometimes be a blunt instrument. 

Spectrix aims to take a more surgical approach by moving into the frequency domain. By utilizing Fast Fourier Transforms (FFT), the plugin breaks down the audio signal into its constituent frequencies (spectral bins). This allows you to apply dynamic processing—such as compression or expansion—independently to different parts of the frequency spectrum.

![Spectrix UI](/images/spectrix.png)

### Technologies Used

To bring this to life, I utilized a robust audio programming stack:

1. **C++**: The industry standard for real-time audio processing due to its deterministic performance characteristics.
2. **JUCE 8**: A brilliant framework that provides the heavy lifting for plugin formats (VST3, AU, AUv3) and the graphical user interface.
3. **FFT Algorithms**: To convert the time-domain audio data into the frequency domain and back again.

### Challenges

Working in the spectral domain introduces latency (due to the block size required for the FFT) and requires careful handling of the windowing and overlap-add processes to avoid audible artifacts like "smearing" or "phasing."

Balancing the visual feedback on the UI with the heavy DSP computations running on the audio thread is always an exciting optimization puzzle.
